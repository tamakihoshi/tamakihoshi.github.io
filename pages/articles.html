Academic Writing

What can we learn from fading art forms like bijin-ga, or the lingering memories of World War II? Grounded in the social sciences, my research traces delicate threads—between history and sociology, record and recollections—seeking to illuminate how the past continues to shape the world we inhabit today.

Case Study of Yomitan Village’s Senjikiroku

This study examines how sociopolitical conditions shaped the writing of local war history in Yomitan Village, Okinawa. Drawing on interviews with the local museum administrator, spatial analysis of exhibits, and a close reading of the Wartime Records, it explores how shifting political and social contexts influenced both its form and retelling. A segment of my research is published in the Waseda Review of Socio-Science.

Social Construction of “The Beautiful Woman”

This study investigates the historical formation of the traditional bijin—the “beautiful woman” depicted in bijinga art—and its evolution into contemporary forms. Combining historical analysis, sociolinguistic data from Japanese women’s magazines, and theories from social constructionism and gender studies, the study traces the cultural shifts that shape modern representations of beauty.

Diplomatic Policy Proposal

This diplomatic policy proposal proposes the establishment of an initiative to tackle unresolved historical disputes—particularly the issue of “comfort women” in South Korea. It acknowledges the complexity of collective memory and advises against any “final and irreversible” resolutions, advocating instead for ongoing reflection and shared remembrance. It was presented at the 2024 ROK-US-Japan Youth Trilateral Leadership Summit in Busan.
